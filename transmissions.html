Wiki / Mechanics

Transmissions

Intercepted signals, encrypted secrets, and the Eclipse Syndicate stirring in the dark.

You are not the only one broadcasting. Open channels carry stray transmissions across Hollow Sector. Some are gifts, some are traps, and some are the hidden pirate faction moving against the people who still live here.

Intercepted messages

Your comms array pulls encrypted transmissions off open channels. The first one arrives shortly after you send out your very first ship; more surface as you play. Each one pops up as MESSAGE INTERCEPTED, where you can jump straight to its contents or file it in your Data archive for later.

The Data archive lives on the Info page and lists every message you have intercepted, decoded or not. You can reopen any of them through View all data.

Mail

Not every transmission needs your attention right away. Faction notices — a faction opening a new Initiative, or a thank-you after you answer a distress call — no longer interrupt you with a popup. They collect in a Mail inbox opened from the envelope icon beside your credits at the top of the screen.

The envelope shows a red count when unread mail is waiting; open a message to mark it read. Urgent transmissions — encrypted intercepts to decode and distress calls — still open on arrival, as before.

Decoding

An encrypted message opens as a corrupted signal shown over an audio waveform. Scrub the marker across the wave and rest it on the glowing peak to charge and lock each fragment, revealing the sender, time, and message piece by piece.

There is no tapping or holding — the marker follows your finger, and the peaks decode out of order, so you hunt around the wave rather than sliding straight across. Some transmissions arrive unencrypted and open fully readable, with no waveform to decode.

Fully decode a message with a secret and an action button appears — for example, View cargo location flies the map straight to what it was hiding.

The Eclipse Syndicate

The Eclipse Syndicate is the sector's hidden pirate faction. Once your first transmission has arrived, the Syndicate begins to stir: roughly every hour there is a chance for a single new event to surface, whether you are actively playing or away. You will never face more than one at a time.

Events arrive as transmissions and come in three kinds. Thank-you notes, warnings, and other follow-up messages are just notifications — they never count as an event or block the next one.

Abandoned cargo

An encrypted transmission points to cargo the Syndicate cut loose near a planet. Decode it, tap View cargo location, and the map flies you to a pinging drop you can recover with a freighter like any other salvage. It stays put until you go get it.

Ignore the message and never decode it, and the signal degrades — the lead fades after a day, so a stack of unread coordinates can't jam up your comms.

Planned raid

A decoded strike order reveals that the Syndicate is about to hit a station belonging to one of the four lawful factions. The countdown starts the moment the message arrives — time spent decoding eats into it, so a scrambled warning is a race.

The Syndicate strikes where you can respond. A raid only ever targets a station your fleet can actually reach from your bases, and the countdown is sized so a capable fighter has time to arrive — at least two hours, up to four for a distant target. A raid is never an automatic loss; the fight itself is never a sure win. The tougher the raid's risk, the more it tracks the strength of your fleet.
No fighters yet? You will still get the call — the Syndicate hits a station at your home planet at low risk, giving you time to buy a fighter from the Vendor and fly it out to defend. It is a nudge into this part of the game, not a punishment.

Decoding marks the target on the map with a pulsing red marker showing a risk level (1–10) and the countdown. Your role is to defend: tap the marker and send fighters to hold the line.

Arrival matters. Fighters that cannot reach the target before the countdown hits zero are not assignable — they are greyed out, with their flight time shown so you know why. Combat resolves on arrival under full raid rules, and fighters can be lost.
  • Hold the line — you earn 5 × the raid's risk in reputation with the defended faction, and its command sends a message of thanks.
  • Too late — if no defenders arrive in time the station falls. Its final transmission cuts off mid-sentence, as if the sender didn't make it. Any fighters still in flight turn around and come home, the maintenance already spent.

Call for help

An unencrypted distress bulletin opens fully readable: a station urgently needs N units of a material within four hours and is paying a premium for it — the best price the market could ever offer for that good.

Any delivery counts, from any source. Freight a shipment in, drop off a mining haul, or load a freighter and send it straight to the station — the requesting station appears in the send list flagged with what it still needs. Every unit of the requested material that reaches the station adds up until you hit the total. Meet it and you are paid on the spot, with a message of thanks. Miss the deadline and someone else comes to their aid; you keep payment only for whatever you had already delivered.
Defending stations and answering calls builds reputation with the lawful factions — the same standing that unlocks better contracts, ships, and ship yards. The Syndicate, meanwhile, remembers who stood against it.
