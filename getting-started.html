Getting Started

Your first steps in Hollow Sector — from ƒ15,000 to a working fleet.

Starting Conditions

Every new game assigns you a random home station from a pool of 15 centrally located stations. Your home base is where you sell materials, store cargo, and manage your fleet. You begin with:

  • ƒ15,000 in starting credits
  • No ships — you must buy your first from the Vendor tab
  • Access to three starter ships: SF1000, FX1000, MN1000

Recommended First Steps

Step 1 — Buy a Freight Ship

Head to the Vendor tab and purchase an SF1000 (ƒ10,000). This is your workhorse for early freight contracts. You'll have ƒ5,000 left — enough buffer to cover upkeep on a short run.

Upkeep matters. Every ship accrues upkeep credits per minute while deployed. If a job doesn't cover upkeep, you lose money. Check the maintenance estimate before confirming any dispatch.

Step 2 — Take a Freight Contract

Open the Job Board and filter to Freight. Look for a short-distance, low-SU contract — ideally 1 SU containers within 500 WU. The SF1000 carries 3 SU and has a 500 WU range, so don't accept jobs beyond that distance.

Job ratings run A through F. Early jobs will mostly be D and F rated — that's normal. Focus on net profit, not rating.

Step 3 — Add a Fighter (Optional but Wise)

An FX1000 (ƒ5,000) provides 2 points of risk aversion. Assign it as escort on freight jobs to reduce your attack failure chance. For Risk 0–1 jobs it's not essential, but it becomes critical as cargo value increases.

Step 4 — Try Mining

An MN1000 (ƒ10,000) can mine common-tier asteroids nearby. Open the Map, tap an asteroid close to your home station, and dispatch. Common materials (Iron, Nickel, Cobalt) have low piracy risk and sell for ƒ1,200/CU at base market rates.

Common Early Mistakes

Forgetting that range costs extra. A route longer than a ship's range is still flyable — the ship chains through stations to get there — but each hop adds a docking fee and time. Check the range stat before dispatching so a short-range hull doesn't rack up fees on a long haul.
Ignoring upkeep on long runs. A long haul on a slow ship can cost more in upkeep than the contract pays. Always check the maintenance estimate in the job detail screen.
Selling everything at once. The market has supply depression — selling large volumes of the same material at the same station drives the price toward zero until the daily reset.
